Legitimacy

On Kingship, Legitimacy, and the Origins of the Claim

The claim associated with Orestis II may be understood through a broader framework of legitimacy, drawing from merit, public service, institutional renewal, ancestral continuity, and civilizational responsibility.

Historical Sources of Legitimacy

The question of political legitimacy is among the oldest questions of statecraft. Throughout history, political authority has been justified through several major principles.

Source of legitimacy Primary basis
Dynastic Historical continuity and succession
Democratic Popular consent and elections
Meritocratic Competence, wisdom, and capability
Foundational Creation, renewal, or transformation of political institutions

The Orestisist Theory of Legitimacy

Orestisism rejects the notion that legitimacy is determined solely by inheritance or by electoral popularity.

An incompetent heir does not become capable through inheritance, just as popularity alone does not guarantee wisdom or competence.

Competence alone, however, is also insufficient. Many capable individuals remain private citizens.

Political legitimacy emerges when competence is joined with public service, institution-building, civilizational vision, constitutional responsibility, and voluntary recognition by the people.

A crown gains meaning not from the metal from which it is forged, but from the burdens carried beneath it.

The Greek Monarchy: Crisis and Solution

More than five centuries have passed since the fall of Constantinople. The institutions of Byzantium disappeared, and many noble families became extinct, dispersed, or faded into historical obscurity. As a consequence, claims of direct dynastic continuity are often difficult to verify officially and rarely capable of establishing legitimacy with complete certainty.

The modern Kingdom of Greece emerged in the nineteenth century under foreign dynasties invited to govern the newly independent state. Whatever one’s view of that institution, the monarchy formally ended following the referendum of 1974 and no longer serves as an active political center.

Greece therefore finds itself in an unusual constitutional position: neither the Byzantine imperial tradition nor the modern Greek monarchy continues as an active constitutional institution.

Orestisism argues that, should a constitutional monarchy ever re-emerge in Greece, its legitimacy would be strongest not through the restoration of an extinct political order, but through the founding of a new constitutional dynasty grounded in merit, public service, institution-building, and democratic recognition. The claim associated with Orestis II should therefore be understood as that of a constitutional founder rather than a dynastic successor.

Every dynasty begins with a founder. Every royal house was once established by an individual whose legitimacy derived not from inheritance but from achievement. History repeatedly demonstrates that periods of institutional decline or political transformation may produce founders who establish entirely new political orders. Figures such as Augustus, Liu Bang, Basil I, Hugh Capet, and Osman I illustrate different historical examples of founders whose legitimacy developed through the creation of new institutions rather than uninterrupted inheritance.

Every dynasty begins with a founder. Every constitutional order begins with an act of creation.

The Claim of Orestis II

The theory developed above may be applied to the present case. The claim associated with Orestis II rests upon four complementary foundations:

  • Meritocratic legitimacy: demonstrated intellectual and academic performance.
  • Foundational-civilizational legitimacy: institutional renewal and civilizational reconstruction.
  • Ancestral legitimacy: family traditions of noble Byzantine descent.
  • Democratic legitimacy: civic participation and public service.

None of these foundations, considered in isolation, is sufficient to establish political legitimacy. Together, however, they constitute the philosophical basis of the claim associated with Orestis II.

The Role of Monarchy in Orestisism

Orestisism does not hold that national renewal depends exclusively upon the restoration of the monarchy. The monarch is understood as merely one permanent constitutional institution among several, intended to represent continuity, national identity, and responsibility toward future generations. A civilization-state governed with wisdom, constitutional restraint, institutional excellence, and long-term stewardship could achieve the same ends via a different constitutional arrangement.

In any case, the Orestean conception of monarchy is not based upon the restoration of historical absolutism or hereditary privilege. Under the proposed constitutional framework, all institutions derive their authority from the Constitution, and ultimate sovereignty remains with the Hellenic people.

The question is therefore not whether Greece can prosper without a monarch, but whether a constitutional Crown could contribute to the long-term continuity, strategic stability, and institutional resilience of the civilization-state.

Final Observation

Whether the ideas presented here remain merely a political philosophy or eventually influence the constitutional development of Greece is a question for history - and ultimately for the Hellenic people - to decide. No individual can declare himself legitimate by proclamation alone.

"The best fortress which a prince can possess is the affection of his people." - Niccolò Machiavelli
